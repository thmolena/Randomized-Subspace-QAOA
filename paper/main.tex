\begin{abstract}
Multi-angle QAOA assigns independent cost and mixer angles, yielding
$d=p(|E|+|V|)$ parameters.  Randomized Subspace QAOA (RSQ) optimizes within a
matrix-free approximation to an observable-Jacobian row space and monitors
discarded sensitivity with probes.  A forward-only variant combines
finite-difference Jacobian actions with simultaneous perturbation.  We establish
a gradient row-space identity, a probe-confidence envelope, and local guarantees
conditional on the true residual.  On 36 paired exact-statevector MaxCut runs
over 14 topologies, RSQ at tolerance $10^{-2}$ reduces optimized dimension by
$39.1\%$ at depth one and $69.6\%$ at depth two.  Paired approximation-ratio
changes are $+0.48\pm0.94$ and $-4.77\pm2.47$ percentage points, with descriptive
uncertainty over 14 topology clusters per depth.  All scheduled checks trigger
rebuilding, and access accounting shows setup overhead.  The results establish
compression with a depth-dependent quality trade-off, not adaptive timing,
hardware scaling, or query advantage.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) alternates a cost
Hamiltonian and a mixer, then adjusts their angles with a classical optimizer
\citep{farhi2014qaoa,hadfield2019quantum,zhou2020qaoa}.  Multi-angle QAOA
(ma-QAOA) replaces the two shared angles in each layer by one phase parameter per
cost term and one mixer parameter per qubit \citep{herrman2022multiangle}.  This
removes a strong tying constraint, but it changes the classical search dimension
from $2p$ to
\begin{equation}
 d=p(|E|+|V|).
 \label{eq:dimension}
\end{equation}
The added coordinates are useful only when they create independent changes in
measurable observables.  Searching all of them indiscriminately can therefore
spend optimization effort in directions to which the circuit is locally
insensitive.

Prior QAOA dimension-reduction methods act on different mathematical objects.
Symmetry-aware tying uses graph automorphisms to identify redundant ma-QAOA
angles and has preserved objective quality on many symmetric instances
\citep{shi2022notallangles}.  ANASTAARS instead constructs adaptive random
Johnson--Lindenstrauss search subspaces, fits interpolation models, reuses
measurements, and adjusts trust regions to estimated noise
\citep{dzahini2025anastaars}.  Hamiltonian-guided leverage embedding compresses
weighted Ising feature matrices assembled from measurement samples before a
reduced trust-region search \citep{mukherjee2026hgle}.  Kernel PCA learns a
nonlinear, cross-instance manifold of promising QAOA parameters from training
graphs \citep{brahmandam2026kpca}, while low-rank tensor completion reconstructs
variational landscapes from partial observations \citep{hao2024landscape}.

RSQ targets a narrower object: the local parameter-space right singular
subspace of the vector-valued observable Jacobian $\J(\bm\theta)$.  It accesses
that operator through JVPs and VJPs (or JVPs alone), and monitors the residual
sensitivity in the orthogonal complement as the anchor moves.  This distinction
specifies the operator and diagnostic; it does not assert priority for
subspace optimization in QAOA.  Classical active subspaces are commonly defined
from a gradient outer product averaged over an input distribution
\citep{constantine2014active,constantine2015active}.  We therefore use
\emph{local observable-sensitivity subspace} for the row space of a
vector-valued Jacobian at one anchor; it is local, observable-dependent, and not
a distribution-averaged global active subspace.

RSQ combines an observable-space formulation with adaptive matrix-free
randomized linear algebra.  The numerical primitive is fixed-tolerance
randomized QB, which approximates a linear operator through products, estimates
its residual from random probes, and chooses its output rank adaptively
\citep{halko2011finding,yu2018randqb,martinsson2020randomized}.
Our contribution is not a new general-purpose QB factorization or a priority
claim for low-dimensional QAOA optimization.  It is the specific coupling of a
local observable-Jacobian operator, matrix-free sensitivity actions, randomized
residual monitoring, and recycled basis updates.  A forward-only realization
replaces adjoint actions during construction, monitoring, and optimization.
The experiments count the setup cost introduced by this coupling instead of
treating dimension reduction as a proxy for resource savings.

The central empirical question is not whether a subspace can be constructed,
but whether the resulting compression preserves optimization quality after its
setup cost is counted.  We answer that question on a deliberately small,
fully released benchmark.  The answer is conditional: at depth one, RSQ closely
tracks full ma-QAOA while optimizing substantially fewer coordinates; at depth
two, stronger compression incurs a measurable quality loss; and on the studied
simulator the operator setup is more expensive than direct optimization.  This
claim boundary is a result, because it separates structural compression from an
unsubstantiated hardware-speedup claim.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item We formulate the per-edge observable Jacobian as a matrix-free operator
and prove that its row space contains the local gradient of any weighted
MaxCut objective.
\item We give a residual-gated optimizer with a recycled update that can
rotate a saturated basis: old directions are augmented by current residual
directions and the combined space is recompressed.  The public result exposes
every residual check, refresh, retained rank, operator count, final iterate and
best observed iterate rather than hiding adaptive decisions behind one score.
\item We give an end-to-end forward-only path: finite-difference JVPs construct
and refresh the basis, and two-evaluation SPSA updates the reduced coordinates.
A dedicated test fails if reverse-mode differentiation is invoked.
\item We release paired results, operator counters, tests, figures, and the
script that regenerates every aggregate in this article.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Observable-space formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}

Let $G=(V,E)$ be an unweighted graph and let
$C_e=(I-Z_iZ_j)/2$ for $e=(i,j)$.  A depth-$p$ ma-QAOA state is
\begin{equation}
 |\psi(\bm\theta)\rangle=
 \prod_{\ell=1}^{p}
 \left[\prod_{i\in V}e^{-i\beta_{\ell i}X_i}
 \prod_{e\in E}e^{-i\gamma_{\ell e}C_e}\right]
 |+\rangle^{\otimes |V|}.
 \label{eq:state}
\end{equation}
Define the vector of local observables
\begin{equation}
 F(\bm\theta)=
 \big(\langle\psi(\bm\theta)|C_e|\psi(\bm\theta)\rangle\big)_{e\in E}
 \in\R^{m}, \qquad m=|E|,
\end{equation}
and its Jacobian
\begin{equation}
 \J(\bm\theta)=\frac{\partial F}{\partial\bm\theta}
 \in\R^{m\times d}.
 \label{eq:jacobian}
\end{equation}
For edge weights $w\in\R^m$, the expected cut is
$C(\bm\theta)=w^{\mathsf T}F(\bm\theta)$.

\begin{proposition}[The local objective gradient is active]
\label{prop:gradient}
At every differentiable point,
\begin{equation}
 \nabla C(\bm\theta)=\J(\bm\theta)^{\mathsf T}w
 \in \operatorname{range}(\J(\bm\theta)^{\mathsf T}).
\end{equation}
Consequently, the steepest first-order change of any weighted sum of the chosen
observables lies in the parameter-space row span of $\J$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Writing $C(\bm\theta)=\sum_{e=1}^{m}w_eF_e(\bm\theta)$, its $j$th
partial derivative is
$\partial_j C=\sum_e w_e\partial_jF_e$.  The $j$th component of
$\J^{\mathsf T}w$ is the same sum.  Hence
$\nabla C=\J^{\mathsf T}w$, which belongs to the range of
$\J^{\mathsf T}$ by construction.
\end{proof}

This statement is local.  The row space may rotate as optimization moves, which
is why a fixed subspace can fail even if it is exact at its construction point.
It also gives an algebraic ceiling
\begin{equation}
 \operatorname{rank}(\J)\leq \min(m,d).
 \label{eq:rankceiling}
\end{equation}
For the observable choice used here, $m$ does not grow with QAOA depth while
$d=p(m+n)$ does.  Depth can therefore increase the compressible complement even
without additional numerical singular-value decay.

\subsection{Randomized Subspace QAOA}
\label{sec:method}

\subsubsection{Matrix-free sensitivity actions}

At an anchor $\bm\theta_0$, RSQ exposes two actions without materializing
$\J$:
\begin{align}
 \operatorname{jvp}(v)&=\J(\bm\theta_0)v,\\
 \operatorname{vjp}(u)&=\J(\bm\theta_0)^{\mathsf T}u.
\end{align}
The implementation evaluates the VJP by reverse-mode automatic differentiation.
It evaluates a JVP either by automatic differentiation or by the central
finite difference
\begin{equation}
 \J v = \frac{F(\bm\theta_0+h v)-F(\bm\theta_0-h v)}{2h}+O(h^2),
 \label{eq:fd}
\end{equation}
with $h=10^{-4}$.  The released tests compare both actions to a dense Jacobian
and verify the adjoint identity.

\subsubsection{Adaptive basis and residual diagnostics}

RSQ factors $A=\J^{\mathsf T}$ as $A\approx \Q B$, where
$\Q\in\R^{d\times r}$ has orthonormal columns and
$B=\Q^{\mathsf T}A$.  Randomized QB expands $\Q$ in blocks until an estimated
relative residual falls below $\varepsilon$ or the algebraic rank ceiling is
reached; the return value distinguishes these stopping conditions.  We expose a Frobenius indicator
and a power-iteration spectral indicator, following established randomized
matrix-free approximation \citep{halko2011finding,yu2018randqb,musco2015randomized}.  The optimization
variables are $z\in\R^r$ with
\begin{equation}
 \bm\theta=\bm\theta_0+\Q z.
 \label{eq:reduced}
\end{equation}

Every $K=25$ steps, RSQ evaluates the residual at the current point.  If the
relative residual exceeds $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{refresh}}=0.05$, it reanchors.
A naive recycled implementation can freeze: if the old basis already has the
maximum requested rank, no new column can enter even when the residual is
large.  Our update instead permits a temporary augmented basis, adds current
residual directions, and takes the singular value decomposition of the small
coefficient matrix $B$.  Rotating by its leading left singular vectors returns
the best requested-rank basis available in the augmented space.  This step is
both implemented and regression tested.

The implementation records the step and value of every randomized residual
check, every triggered refresh, the retained-rank trajectory, and forward,
JVP and VJP counts separately.  It returns both the final iterate used by the
released tables and the best objective value observed along the trajectory.
This distinction prevents a user-facing deployment from silently discarding a
better intermediate point while preserving the final-iterate convention of the
paired experiment.

\subsubsection{Forward-only construction, refresh and optimization}

The forward-only variant selects an $r$-dimensional trial space by applying JVPs
to a Gaussian parameter sketch and solving the resulting small Rayleigh--Ritz
problem.  Its refresh diagnostic also uses only JVPs.  For independent
$v_i\sim\mathcal N(0,I_d)$, define
\begin{equation}
 \widehat\rho_F^2=
 \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{s}\norm{\J(\I-\projector)v_i}^2}
      {\sum_{i=1}^{s}\norm{\J v_i}^2}.
 \label{eq:forwarddiagnostic}
\end{equation}
Forward differences evaluate both numerator and denominator actions.  The identity
$\E\norm{Mv}^2=\norm{M}_F^2$ implies that the numerator and denominator
converge to the corresponding squared Frobenius norms as $s$ grows.  We use
$s=12$.  Unlike a spectral indicator, Eq.~\eqref{eq:forwarddiagnostic}
controls average discarded sensitivity; a missed worst direction remains a
failure mode.

Reduced optimization also avoids reverse-mode access.  For
$L(z)=-C(\bm\theta_0+\Q z)$ and a Rademacher vector
$\delta_t\in\{-1,1\}^r$, it uses
\begin{equation}
 \widehat g_t=
 \frac{L(z_t+c\delta_t)-L(z_t-c\delta_t)}{2c}\,\delta_t,
 \qquad c=10^{-4}.
 \label{eq:spsa}
\end{equation}
If $L$ has bounded third derivatives, Taylor expansion and
$\E[\delta_{ti}\delta_{tj}]=\mathbf{1}\{i=j\}$ give
$\E[\widehat g_t\mid z_t]=\nabla L(z_t)+O(c^2)$
componentwise \citep{spall1992spsa}.  To leading order, its $i$th component is
\begin{equation}
 \widehat g_{ti}=\partial_iL(z_t)
 +\sum_{j\neq i}\partial_jL(z_t)\delta_{ti}\delta_{tj}+O(c^2),
 \label{eq:spsa-leading}
\end{equation}
so a single noiseless estimate has
$\operatorname{Var}(\widehat g_{ti}\mid z_t)
=\sum_{j\neq i}(\partial_jL(z_t))^2+O(c^2)$: decreasing $c$ removes leading
finite-difference bias but not the cross-coordinate perturbation variance.
If the two objective evaluations instead have independent, zero-mean errors of
variance $\sigma^2$, their contribution to each component variance is
$\sigma^2/(2c^2)$.  Shot noise therefore creates the usual bias--variance
trade-off; correlated sampling, repeated perturbations, and a scheduled $c$
may help, but require a separate analysis.  The released exact-statevector
experiment has $\sigma^2=0$.  Equation~\eqref{eq:spsa} costs two objective
evaluations independently of $r$, but it is not an exact gradient, and the
finite run supplies no asymptotic SPSA convergence claim.

\begin{proposition}[Moment identity for the forward residual diagnostic]
\label{prop:residual-moments}
Let $P=\projector$, let $R=\J(\I-P)$, and let
$v_1,\ldots,v_s$ be independent standard Gaussian
vectors.  For
\begin{equation}
 \widehat a_s=\frac{1}{s}\sum_{i=1}^{s}\norm{Rv_i}^{2},
\end{equation}
we have
\begin{equation}
 \E[\widehat a_s]=\norm{R}_F^2,
 \qquad
 \operatorname{Var}(\widehat a_s)
 =\frac{2}{s}\norm{R^{\mathsf T}R}_F^2.
 \label{eq:residual-moments}
\end{equation}
The analogous identities hold for the denominator of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:forwarddiagnostic} with $R$ replaced by $\J$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Set $H=R^{\mathsf T}R$.  For a standard real Gaussian vector $v$,
$\E[vv^{\mathsf T}]=\I$, so
$\E[v^{\mathsf T}Hv]=\operatorname{tr}(H)=\norm{R}_F^2$.
Isserlis' identity gives
$\E[(v^{\mathsf T}Hv)^2]=\operatorname{tr}(H)^2+2\operatorname{tr}(H^2)$;
therefore its variance is
$2\operatorname{tr}(H^2)=2\norm{R^{\mathsf T}R}_F^2$.
The $s$ summands are independent, so the variance of their mean is divided by
$s$.  Replacing $R$ by $\J$ proves the denominator statement.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:residual-moments} justifies the diagnostic as a consistent
randomized estimator.  The following result states what can be concluded at a
finite probe count without assuming a stable rank.

\begin{proposition}[Finite-probe confidence envelope]
\label{prop:probe-envelope}
Let $a=\norm{R}_F^2>0$, $b=\norm{\J}_F^2>0$, and let
$\widehat\rho_F=(\widehat a_s/\widehat b_s)^{1/2}$ be formed from Gaussian
quadratic-form averages as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:forwarddiagnostic}.  For
$\delta\in(0,1)$ set $t=2/\sqrt{s\delta}$.  If $t<1$, then, with probability at
least $1-\delta$,
\begin{equation}
 \sqrt{\frac{1-t}{1+t}}\,\widehat\rho_F
 \leq \frac{\norm{R}_F}{\norm{\J}_F}
 \leq
 \sqrt{\frac{1+t}{1-t}}\,\widehat\rho_F.
 \label{eq:probe-envelope}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
For either quadratic-form average, Proposition~\ref{prop:residual-moments} and
$\norm{M^{\mathsf T}M}_F^2\leq\norm{M}_F^4$ give relative variance at most
$2/s$.  Chebyshev's inequality with radius $t$ fails with probability at most
$2/(st^2)=\delta/2$ for each of numerator and denominator.  A union bound makes
both relative bounds simultaneous.  Taking the worst ratio and then a square
root yields Eq.~\eqref{eq:probe-envelope}.
\end{proof}

The envelope is intentionally conservative.  At the released $s=12$ and
$95\%$ confidence, $t>1$, so it is non-informative: the observed ratio is a
refresh diagnostic, not a run-wise confidence certificate.  More than 80
probes are required before this elementary $95\%$ envelope is finite, and a
usefully tight interval requires substantially more.  The ratio is not
unbiased, no finite probe count gives a deterministic upper bound, and central
finite differences add the $O(h^2)$ error of Eq.~\eqref{eq:fd}.  Accordingly,
Theorem~\ref{thm:loss} is conditional on the true operator residual.

\begin{proposition}[True-relative-residual bridge]
\label{prop:residual-gradient}
Let $P=\projector$ be an orthogonal projector and suppose
$\norm{\J(\bm\theta)}_F>0$.  Define the true relative Frobenius residual
\begin{equation}
 \rho_F(\bm\theta,P)=
 \frac{\norm{\J(\bm\theta)(\I-P)}_F}{\norm{\J(\bm\theta)}_F}.
 \label{eq:true-relative-residual}
\end{equation}
For $C=w^{\mathsf T}F$ and $g=\nabla C(\bm\theta)$,
\begin{equation}
 \norm{g-Pg}
 \leq \rho_F(\bm\theta,P)\norm{\J(\bm\theta)}_F\norm{w}.
 \label{eq:gradient-residual-bridge}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Proposition~\ref{prop:gradient}, symmetry of $P$, and $P^2=P$ give
$(\I-P)g=[\J(\I-P)]^{\mathsf T}w$.  Hence
\begin{align*}
 \norm{g-Pg}
 &\leq \norm{\J(\I-P)}_2\norm{w}\\
 &\leq \norm{\J(\I-P)}_F\norm{w}
 =\rho_F\norm{\J}_F\norm{w}.
\end{align*}
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:residual-gradient} is the bridge from the true relative
quantity approximated by Eq.~\eqref{eq:forwarddiagnostic} to an objective-level
statement.  Substituting the finite-probe ratio for $\rho_F$ is not justified
without an accompanying probabilistic envelope.

\subsubsection{Local consequences of a true residual bound}

\begin{theorem}[True-residual projected-step discrepancy]
\label{thm:loss}
Let $P=\projector$ and suppose
$\norm{\J(\bm\theta)(\I-P)}_2\leq\varepsilon$.  Let $C=w^{\mathsf T}F$ be twice
continuously differentiable on a convex neighborhood containing the segments
from $\bm\theta$ to both $\bm\theta+\Delta$ and $\bm\theta+P\Delta$, and suppose
$\norm{\nabla^2 C(\xi)}_2\leq L_C$ throughout that neighborhood.  Then
\begin{equation}
 \left|C(\bm\theta+\Delta)-C(\bm\theta+P\Delta)\right|
 \leq \norm{w}\,\varepsilon\norm{\Delta}+L_C\norm{\Delta}^2.
 \label{eq:lossbound}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Taylor's theorem gives
$C(\bm\theta+\Delta)=C(\bm\theta)+\nabla C(\bm\theta)^{\mathsf T}\Delta+r_1$
with $|r_1|\leq L_C\norm{\Delta}^2/2$, and the analogous expansion along
$P\Delta$ has $|r_2|\leq L_C\norm{P\Delta}^2/2\leq
L_C\norm{\Delta}^2/2$.  Proposition~\ref{prop:gradient} makes the linear
difference $w^{\mathsf T}\J(\bm\theta)(\I-P)\Delta$.  Cauchy--Schwarz and the
assumed operator-norm residual bound give magnitude at most
$\norm{w}\varepsilon\norm{\Delta}$.  Adding $|r_1|+|r_2|$ proves the claim.
\end{proof}

The theorem does not give global convergence or an approximation-ratio
guarantee.  It states exactly what a true residual bound controls: the
first-order change discarded at the current anchor.

\begin{theorem}[One exact projected-gradient ascent step]
\label{thm:projected-ascent}
Let $P=\projector$, let $g=\nabla C(\bm\theta)$, and suppose $\nabla C$ is
$L$-Lipschitz on the segment from $\bm\theta$ to
$\bm\theta^+=\bm\theta+\alpha Pg$, with $L>0$ and $0<\alpha\leq1/L$.
Then
\begin{equation}
 C(\bm\theta^+)\geq C(\bm\theta)
 +\alpha\left(1-\frac{L\alpha}{2}\right)\norm{Pg}^2
 \geq C(\bm\theta)+\frac{\alpha}{2}\norm{Pg}^2.
 \label{eq:projected-ascent}
\end{equation}
If $\norm{\J(\bm\theta)}_F>0$, the omitted gradient component additionally
satisfies Eq.~\eqref{eq:gradient-residual-bridge}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The smoothness inequality applied to
$s=\bm\theta^+-\bm\theta=\alpha Pg$ gives
\begin{align*}
 C(\bm\theta+s)
 &\geq C(\bm\theta)+g^{\mathsf T}s-\frac{L}{2}\norm{s}^2\\
 &=C(\bm\theta)+\alpha\norm{Pg}^2
   -\frac{L\alpha^2}{2}\norm{Pg}^2,
\end{align*}
where $g^{\mathsf T}Pg=\norm{Pg}^2$ because $P$ is an orthogonal projector.
The first inequality in Eq.~\eqref{eq:projected-ascent} follows; the step-size
condition gives the second.  The final statement is
Proposition~\ref{prop:residual-gradient}.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:projected-ascent} is deliberately a one-step result for exact
projected-gradient ascent with a fixed projector.  The released two-sided path
uses Adam, the forward-only path feeds the estimator in Eq.~\eqref{eq:spsa} to
Adam, and both paths may replace $P$ after a randomized residual check.  The
theorem is therefore not a convergence guarantee for Adam, SPSA, finite-probe
residual estimates, or a moving randomized basis.

\paragraph{Algorithm.}
The implemented loop is:
\begin{enumerate}
\item construct $\Q$ at $\bm\theta_0$ to tolerance $\varepsilon$ or the rank
ceiling, and record the stopping status;
\item update $z$ for the objective $-C(\bm\theta_0+\Q z)$, using Adam for the
two-sided path or a two-evaluation Rademacher SPSA estimate for the forward-only
path \citep{kingma2015adam,spall1992spsa};
\item every $K$ steps, estimate the current residual;
\item when it exceeds threshold, reanchor, augment and recompress the recycled
basis, reset $z$, and continue.
\end{enumerate}
Standalone observable evaluations, JVPs and VJPs are instrumented as overlapping
categories; ranks, residual checks and refreshes are recorded separately.

\subsection{Access and computational complexity}

Let $r$ be the retained rank, $b$ the QB block size, $q$ the number of expansion
blocks, $s_0$ the initial norm-probe count and $s$ the residual-probe count.
Ignoring pruning, the Frobenius two-sided build uses
$s_0+(q+1)s+qb$ VJPs and $qb$ JVPs.  A two-sided refresh of an existing rank-$r$
basis uses $r$ JVPs to form its coefficient matrix and $2s$ VJPs for the residual
ratio.  This describes the diagnostic itself; a triggered rebuild incurs its
own norm probes, residual probes, coefficient updates and any expansion blocks.
The forward-only build uses $k=\min(d,r+\text{oversampling})$ JVPs; a standalone
forward-only residual check uses $2s$ JVPs.  A central-difference JVP costs two
observable-map evaluations.  Each forward-only SPSA optimization step uses two
objective evaluations, followed by one final-iterate evaluation at the end of
the run.  These exact
categories are emitted in the released CSV; they overlap and must not be added
as if they were disjoint physical-shot costs.

For the bundled exact simulator, one observable-map evaluation takes
$O(p(m+n)2^n)$ arithmetic and stores $O((m+1)2^n)$ numbers because all per-edge
cut indicators are precomputed.  This backend is a small-instance reference, not
a large-scale simulator.  On hardware, one computational-basis sample can update
all commuting MaxCut edge observables, but shot complexity depends on the desired
joint precision and noise model; neither a shot bound nor a circuit-depth
advantage is claimed here.  The matrix-free callback interface permits external
backends without changing the QB core.

\subsection{Experimental protocol}
\label{sec:experiments}

We test unweighted MaxCut on three graph families: random 3-regular graphs,
Erdos--Renyi graphs with edge probability $0.5$, and rings.  The grid contains
$n\in\{8,10\}$, $p\in\{1,2\}$, and three restart identifiers: 36 paired
optimizer runs.  Random regular and Erdos--Renyi generation yields 12 distinct
graphs; the two rings repeat across restart identifiers, giving 14 unique
topologies and 28 topology--depth clusters.  Graph generation, angle
initialization and randomized linear algebra use separately recorded seeds
($0$--$2$, $100000$--$100002$, and $200000$--$200002$, respectively).  A
canonical edge list and SHA-256-derived graph identifier accompany every row.
The simulator is a pure-PyTorch, complex-128 statevector implementation
\citep{paszke2019pytorch}; an optional PennyLane backend supplies an independent
cross-check \citep{bergholm2018pennylane}.  Brute-force MaxCut gives the exact
denominator for the approximation ratio.

Every optimizer receives 100 steps at learning rate $0.05$.  Full ma-QAOA,
the fixed-rank oracle and both randomized variants share the same run-specific
initial angle vector.  The symmetry baseline begins at its orbit-wise mean
projection, so it is derived from the same vector while satisfying its tying
constraints.  Adam is used except for the forward-only SPSA update
\citep{kingma2015adam,spall1992spsa}.  We
compare:
\begin{itemize}
\item full ma-QAOA;
\item angles tied by graph-automorphism orbits;
\item a rank-8 dense-SVD oracle fixed at the initial Jacobian (its materialization
is recorded separately rather than disguised as a matrix-free action);
\item two-sided adaptive RSQ at $\varepsilon\in\{10^{-1},10^{-2}\}$;
\item forward-only rank-8 RSQ.
\end{itemize}
The primary outcome is expected-cut approximation ratio.  Parameter count is
the dimension optimized after construction.  Paired differences subtract the
full ma-QAOA ratio on the same graph, initialization and sketch identifier.
Repeated restarts of an identical topology are averaged within each
topology--depth cluster before analysis.  We report the mean and sample standard
deviation across 14 cluster means per depth for raw ratios, and the mean $\pm$
two standard errors across the corresponding paired cluster means.  These are
descriptive summaries, not independent-instance hypothesis tests; no absence of
significance is interpreted as equivalence.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Depth one: compression with a small paired change}

At $p=1$, full ma-QAOA optimizes $23.7$ parameters on average and attains
$0.862\pm0.053$.  RSQ at $\varepsilon=10^{-2}$ optimizes $14.7$ parameters, a
$39.1\%$ reduction, and attains $0.867\pm0.051$.  The paired change is
$+0.48\pm0.94$ percentage points.  The two-standard-error bar spans zero, but
the experiment was not designed as a formal equivalence trial; this grid does
not resolve a directional average change at depth one.

The rank-8 fixed-subspace oracle reaches $0.764\pm0.051$, a paired change of
$-9.77\pm2.09$ points.  Forward-only rank-8 RSQ reaches
$0.798\pm0.055$, improving on the fixed oracle but remaining
$6.39\pm1.98$ points below full ma-QAOA.  The comparison is consistent with a
benefit from a moving subspace, but it does not isolate refresh from differences
in construction and optimization.

\subsection{Depth two: the failure boundary becomes visible}

At $p=2$, full ma-QAOA optimizes $47.4$ parameters on average and attains
$0.945\pm0.044$.  RSQ optimizes the same $14.7$-dimensional observable-controlled
subspace as at depth one, now a $69.6\%$ reduction.  At tolerance $10^{-2}$ its
ratio is $0.897\pm0.054$, a paired change of $-4.77\pm2.47$ points.  The added
layer grows the ambient parameter space without growing the observable count,
but the more aggressive compression no longer preserves the full optimizer's
quality.

The forward-only rank-8 variant loses $14.73\pm2.65$ points at depth two.  The
fixed rank-8 oracle loses $18.07\pm2.54$ points.  These results rule out a blanket
claim that a very small initial sensitivity space is sufficient for ma-QAOA.
They do not identify which component of the moving-subspace pipeline causes the
difference and may indicate a need for richer observables at higher depth.

\subsection{The residual indicator identifies algebraic, not extra numerical, rank}

Across the tested tolerances, two-sided RSQ retains $14.7$ directions on average,
the average number of edge observables.  Tightening the tolerance from $0.1$ to
$0.01$ does not change the mean retained rank, although the paired quality
summaries differ modestly.  Five of 144 builds at tolerance $0.01$ and three of
144 builds at tolerance $0.1$ reached the algebraic rank ceiling before their
finite-probe indicator met the requested tolerance.  Thus this benchmark
does not show additional numerical low-rank decay beyond the algebraic ceiling
in Eq.~\eqref{eq:rankceiling}.  What grows with depth is the complement between
$m$ and $d$, not a newly discovered decay of the nonzero spectrum.

\subsection{Setup cost prevents a query-efficiency claim}

At the primary tolerance $0.01$, the two-sided method averages 749 forward-$F$
evaluations, 134 JVPs, and 481 VJPs at $p=1$, compared with 101 forward
evaluations and 100 VJPs for the full baseline under the simulator's accounting.
At $p=2$ the corresponding RSQ counts increase to 867, 155, and 556.  The
forward-only path uses no VJP, as intended, but requires about 488--489 forward
evaluations and 143--144 JVPs.
These categories are instrumentation counters, not additive physical-shot
counts; a JVP by central difference contains forward evaluations already
recorded by the lower-level counter.

\section{Discussion}

The experiments support a narrower and more useful conclusion than the original
resource-efficiency hypothesis.  At depth one, the observable row space removes
roughly two fifths of the optimized coordinates with little resolved change in
mean quality.  At depth two, the same observable dimension removes roughly seven
tenths of the coordinates but loses about five approximation-ratio points.
Parameter compression is therefore real, while quality preservation is
depth-dependent.

The fixed-rank oracle underperforms the adaptive pipeline, which is consistent
with a local row space that changes along the trajectory.  That comparison does
not, however, isolate the refresh gate: basis construction, rank, and optimizer
also differ.  Moreover, 324 of 324 scheduled residual checks across the two
tolerances and the forward-only variant exceeded the threshold and triggered a
rebuild.  The experiment therefore
evaluates a periodically refreshed implementation; it does not show that the
gate saved rebuilds or selected better refresh times.  A matched-rank comparison
among no-refresh, fixed-schedule, and threshold-gated variants is needed to
identify that effect.  The regression suite separately checks that recycling a
saturated basis can rotate rather than freezing its old span.

The forward-only path establishes feasibility but not parity.  It avoids every
VJP, including during refresh, and outperforms the equally sized fixed oracle.
Its remaining quality loss can arise from restricting the Rayleigh--Ritz search
to a random trial space, finite-difference error, single-perturbation SPSA
variance, and the use of an average Frobenius drift diagnostic.  Because the
released objectives are exact statevector expectations, they do not test the
$1/c^2$ measurement-noise amplification in the SPSA estimator.  Hardware studies
should compare larger sketches, common-random-number shot estimators, repeated
perturbations, gain schedules, and worst-direction forward indicators.

For the fixed scalar objective $C=w^{\mathsf T}F$ used here, the weight vector
$w$ is known and a single VJP returns $\J^{\mathsf T}w=\nabla C$ directly.
Constructing a basis for the larger range of $\J^{\mathsf T}$ therefore has no
demonstrated single-objective gradient-query benefit.  Such a basis could be
amortized across multiple weight vectors, objectives or related instances, but
the released benchmark tests none of those use cases.  Likewise, the
two-evaluation SPSA cost in Eq.~\eqref{eq:spsa} is independent of reduced rank,
but ordinary full-space SPSA also uses two objective evaluations per step.  A
full-space SPSA baseline is absent, so the forward-only results establish
parameter-space compression and zero-VJP execution, not an objective-query
advantage.

Most importantly, fewer optimized coordinates do not automatically mean fewer
quantum queries.  The released small-instance counts go in the opposite
direction because basis construction is expensive.  A genuine query advantage
would require amortizing a basis across related instances, using hardware-native
batching, or reaching a scale at which full-gradient cost grows faster than the
matrix-free sketch.  Those are testable future conditions, not conclusions of
the present data.

\section{Limitations}

The benchmark contains only 36 paired runs on 14 unique topologies,
$n\leq10$, depths one and two, and exact statevector objectives.  It does not
model shot noise, device noise,
compilation, queue latency, or weighted spin glasses.  The graph families are
standard but narrow.  One hundred optimizer steps may favor some parameterizations
over others, and no hyperparameter sweep is used to optimize each baseline
separately.  For the fixed-rank oracle, dense-Jacobian materialization is
included in wall time and recorded as a separate event, but no hardware-query
equivalent is assigned; it is therefore primarily a quality reference.  The
randomized residual ratios are finite-
probe estimators and can underestimate the true residual; no deterministic or
run-wise coverage guarantee is claimed for the chosen probe count.  All 324 of
324 released refresh checks triggered, so no empirical saving from residual gating is
established.  The projected-ascent theorem does not cover the Adam or SPSA
updates used in the experiment, and the exact-statevector setting does not test
shot-noise bias--variance trade-offs.  The study also omits multi-weight
amortization and a full-space SPSA control, both required to test the two
potential query-saving mechanisms described above.  Finally, the observable vector contains
per-edge costs; adding mixer observables, commutators, or layer-resolved features
could raise rank but improve high-depth fidelity.

\section{Reproducibility}

The canonical package is the repository's \texttt{code/} directory.  A clean
environment can install and verify it with
\begin{verbatim}
python -m pip install -e 'code[dev]'
python -m pytest code/tests -q
cd code
python experiments/run_experiment.py \
  --config experiments/configs/maxcut_small.yaml
python experiments/summarize_results.py
python experiments/validate_release.py
\end{verbatim}
The experiment runner writes 216 CSV rows and records separate graph,
initialization and sketch seeds; canonical edge lists and graph hashes; complete
objective trajectories and final parameters; every residual value, refresh
step and basis-build status; deterministic-thread settings; environment versions;
and SHA-256 fingerprints of the implementation, experiment runner and effective
configuration.  The
summarizer reads only that CSV and regenerates the four figures, Table
\ref{tab:summary}, and a machine-readable JSON summary.  Tests cover the
statevector against a dense reference, JVP/VJP consistency, fixed-tolerance
factorization, spectral residual estimation, basis recycling, the forward-only
zero-VJP invariant, best-iterate and residual-audit reporting, invalid-control
rejection, baselines, the command-line JSON contract, and the optional
PennyLane cross-check.  No test-count claim is hard-coded because collection
varies with optional backends.

\section{Data and code availability}

The row-level CSV, machine-readable summary, generated tables and figure source

\section{Conclusion}

RSQ turns the local observable Jacobian of ma-QAOA into an adaptive optimization
space without forming the Jacobian.  Its residual has a precise role: the true
operator residual bounds discarded first-order sensitivity, while its randomized
estimate decides when the space is updated.  On the released benchmark, this mechanism preserves depth-one quality
under substantial parameter compression, reveals a depth-two compression--quality
tradeoff, and carries nontrivial setup cost.  These findings establish a
fact-checkable baseline for future randomized-subspace quantum optimization and
define the evidence required before stronger query-efficiency claims are made.
